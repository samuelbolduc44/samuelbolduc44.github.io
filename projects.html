Julia code to compute rotation numbers of a periodically forced delay differential equation

This project computes the rotation numbers of the pfS&S DDE via stroboscopic-section in a two parameters space. Loosely, the rotation number $\rho$ is the ratio between the frequencies of the two interacting oscillations at play: the external forcing (period $T_{\omega} = 2\pi/\omega$) and the system's own delay-induced oscillation. On an attracting invariant torus, $\rho$ characterizes the type of motion on the torus — rational $\rho = p/q$ corresponds to mode locking (a periodic orbit that closes after $q$ forcing periods), irrational $\rho$ corresponds to quasiperiodic motion that never exactly closes. The key idea is to iterate the Poincar\'{e} map until the final point is in a small neighborhood of the first point on $\mathcal{I}$. The rotation number $\rho$ is then the average angular displacement over one full finite orbit of $\mathcal{P}$. Adapting this approach to the DDE setting faces two new difficulties. First, the return condition cannot be imposed solely on the headpoints in the Poincare section, but must be formulated in the full function space $\mathcal{C}_\tau$ to ensure that entire solution segments are close. Second, since the dynamics evolves in an infinite-dimensional space, a suitable projection onto $\mathbb{R}^2$ is required in order to define and compute the angle increments. The computation of the rotation number $\rho$ of a DDE in parameter space is effective. For instance, the grid in the $(\tau,c)$-plane for the pfS&S model shown in our paper requires $768{,}000$ rotation number computations in total, taking approximately $8$ hours with the third-order Adams--Bashforth method in Julia without parallelization on an Apple M1 (16\,GB).

Compute local maxima of a delay differential equation

This repository contains a Julia script for generating bifurcation diagrams of a scalar (potentially non-autonomous) delay differential equation. The script solves the delay differential equation for a sequence of delay values and records the local maxima of the long-time solution to construct a classical bifurcation diagram.

Current Working Project

Comprehensive bifurcation analysis of the pfSS model

We perform a comprehensive bifurcation analysis of the proposed pfSS model, with the nonlinearity strength treated as the primary continuation parameter to systematically map the system's dynamical landscape. This project is done with the continuation package DDEBIFTOOL in MATLAB. We demonstrate that chaos emerges via overlapping resonance tongues, bringing the model's behavior closer to the variability observed in ENSO. This perspective is consistent with previous studies in which rich dynamics were found in the low-frequency regime.

Generalization of Feedback with implicit state-dependent delay in a conceptual model of El Niño

Generalization of the paper for arbitrarly number of nestings and for arbtrary bounded and measurable functions.

In state-dependent delayed negative feedback systems, the delay is often not a prescribed function of the current state but rather the solution of an implicit algebraic equation[1]. Indeed, consider a feedback signal that departs at time $t$ and returns after a lag $\sigma$, arriving at time $s = t + \sigma$. If the travel time is a reference lag $\bar\tau$ corrected by a state-dependent response at the moment of departure, then $\sigma = \bar\tau+\lambda(h(t))$. But since $t = s - \sigma$, the departure state satisfies $h(t) = h(s-\sigma)$, and the lag equation becomes $\sigma = \bar\tau+\lambda(h(s-\sigma))$. Renaming $s$ by $t$, the delay $\sigma(t)$ satisfies $\sigma(t) = \bar\tau+\lambda(h(t-\sigma(t)))$, and the system takes the form

\begin{equation} \label{eq:ddeimplicit} \left. \begin{aligned} h'(t) &= f(h(t-\sigma(t))), \\ 0 &= \bar\tau+\lambda(h(t-\sigma(t))) - \sigma(t). \end{aligned} \right\} \end{equation}

The delay $\sigma(t)$ determines which past moment $t-\sigma(t)$ is relevant, but the state at that moment in turn determines $\sigma(t)$: neither can be found independently of the other, and both must be determined simultaneously as a self-consistent pair. Our main result focuses on the regime where the implicit fixed-point problem is contractive. In this regime, delayed state dependence for a particular class of piecewise-smooth delay differential equations is dynamically invisible: introducing the implicit delay does not change the solution of the constant-delay problem.

[1] Walther, H.-O. (2014). Topics in delay differential equations. Jahresbericht der Deutschen Mathematiker-Vereinigung, 116(2), 87–114.

Generalization of Seasonal forcing dominated dynamics of a piecewise smooth Ghil-Zaliapin-Thompson ENSO model

Stability of a periodic orbit can be assessed by studying how small perturbations evolve under the dynamics. For a symmetric $n$:$s$-periodic orbit, this reduces to tracking the evolution of the orbit’s nondegenerate zeros. By analyzing how the zeros of a perturbed orbit shift over one period, we obtain a linear recurrence relation.
